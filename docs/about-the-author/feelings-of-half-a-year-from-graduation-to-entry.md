I don't...

If you've seen my introduction before, you'll know I'm one of the millions of graduates who graduated in the 19th year. The article focuses on how I feel about my first six months in the office, and there are a lot of my own subjective feelings. If you disagree with anything, please feel free to share your views in the comment area; I respect the views of others.

Let's talk about our situation. I'm currently in a foreign company, and every day I work as much as most people do. It's been almost six months since graduation, and there are currently two business-oriented projects in the company, one of which is underway. It's hard to imagine that the back end of the two business projects that I'm working on at the company is not related to distributed/microservices, and that I don't have access to Redis, Kafka, and other, more “advanced” technologies in the project.

The first was the internal project of the company — the staff growth system. Forget the name of the staff growth system; actually, what the system does is performance appraisal, like your performance in a project group. The technology for this project is Spring Boot + JPA + Spring Security + K8S + Docker + React. The second is an integrated game (Cocos), a web management end (Spring Boot + Vue), and an applet (Taro) project.

Yes, most of my time at work is related to CRUD, and I write the front page every day. A friend I knew before was very curious when he heard that most of my project was about business code, and he didn't think it would be up to him? What? So, **I wonder why a lot of people who can't even write business code right now get sick when they hear CRUD? At least I feel that during my time at work, I have improved the quality of my code, significantly improved my ability to locate problems, gained a deeper understanding of business, and could do some front-end development on my own.**

Actually, I personally think it's not that easy to write business code and complain that I'm working on CRUD every day before I see my CRUD code. In other words, did you figure out what kind of notes or categories you used to write about CRUD? It's like a simple comment like `@Service`, `@Autowired`, `@RestController`, saying I already have Spring Boot.

The experience of actually using Redis and MQ, which may be relevant to the current interview environment, will be very impressive at some point. You need to be different from other people, and if you want to go to a big company, it's like you have to be better at these techniques. Well, there's no such thing as self-confidence.

**To be honest, I was caught up in this “perjury” in college.** When I was in college, I was in contact with Java because I joined a technical-oriented school, and the purpose of our study was to develop a campus. It took me a while to get to Java when I was in second grade, and it took me some time to master the Java foundation. Then I started studying Android development.

By the third year of school, I really decided to go in the direction of Java backend and look for Java backend development. After learning about three months of web development basics, I started learning about distributional aspects like Redis and Dubbo. I was learning by reading, watching videos, and reading blogs, and I completed two projects by watching videos myself: a normal business system and a distributed system. **I thought I'd done a great job, and I don't think the normal CRUD job is my current level. Ha ha! Now it looks like I was too overconfident!**

This is not! It's a problem when you work with your teacher for the third summer. In the third year, we followed the teacher to a performance appraisal system with a medium level of operational complexity. The technology for this project is: SSM + Shiro + JSP. I was having problems with this project, and I didn't think I'd write code, and even a simple CRUD would take days. So, at that time, I was learning to write while studying. I was tired, but I learned a lot at that time, and it made me even stronger in terms of technology. I think the phrase “**this project is no longer possible to maintain**” is the most negative thing about my project.

Technology is changing, and it is the king who controls the core. We may still have been using Spring, based on traditional XML development in previous years, and now almost everyone will be using Spring Boot. For example, a few years ago, we might still be using ActiveMQ, which is virtually non-existent today. You can't learn from every framework/tool today so quickly.
