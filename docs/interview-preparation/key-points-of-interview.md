# Java, What's the Focus of the Backend Interview?

**What are the main points of knowledge when preparing for interviews? How to focus?**

I'll give you some good advice:

1. Java Foundation, Gathering, Combustion, MySQL, Redis, Spring, Spring Boot, which are the knowledge points necessary for Java backend development (MySQL + Redis >= Java > Spring + Spring Boot). These are the knowledge points more frequently asked in large and small companies. Spring and Spring Boot, the framework knowledge points, are slightly less important than the previous knowledge points, but interviews in general also ask questions, especially in small and medium-sized companies. It is also more difficult to ask questions from large and medium-sized firms in general, especially when they prefer to dig deep and can easily be taken down. The relevant elements of the computer base are mentioned below.
1. The knowledge points involved in your project experience are of the utmost importance, and a qualified interviewer will ask questions in light of your project experience. For example, if your project experience uses Redis to limit flow, then Redis-related eight parts (such as the Redis common data structure) and the eight parts related to restricted flow (such as the usual limit algorithm) should be areas where you spend more time trying to understand. After you've mastered the project's experience, you'll need to focus on your résumé, and then you'll have to spend time preparing other knowledge points.
1. In response to the need to find work for yourself, you can also appropriately refocus your study. Small and medium-sized companies typically ask for a lower computer base, while some large companies, such as ByteDance, place more emphasis on the computer base, especially algorithms. So, if you're targeting small and medium-sized companies, the computer base is less important for interview preparation. If there is not enough time to review, it could be set aside for the time being to focus on other important knowledge points.
1. General school interviews do not impose distributed/micro-service, high-level knowledge (which does not preclude the rigidity of individual jobs) on you, so whether you want to master it depends on your current personal reality. If you have knowledge of this, it would be relatively advantageous for the interview (in order to have a bright spot on the project or to have knowledge of performance optimization). Optimal knowledge is also a small branch of high-level and knowledge-producing knowledge. If your skills introduction or project experience involves distributed/micro-service, high-level knowledge, it is recommended that you take as much time as possible to prepare carefully, as interviews are likely to ask about it, especially when project experience is used. However, the main points of knowledge to be written on the curriculum vitae would be sufficient.
1. JVM-related knowledge points are typically asked by large companies (e.g., the United States, Alibaba) and some good medium-sized companies (e.g., CarryOn, Cash Network), while interviews with state companies and smaller factories do not require as much preparation. The more frequently asked topics include the [Java Memory Area](https://javaguide.cn/java/jvm/mory-area.html), [JVM Garbage Collection](https://javaguide.cn/java/jvm/jvm/garbage-collection.html), [Class Loaders and Parent Delegation Models](https://javaguide.cn/java/jvm/classloader.html), and I have shared a number of [common online cases](https://t.zsxq.com/0bsAac47U) related to JVM.
1. The focus of interviews will also vary from company to company. For example, if you're going to a company like Alibaba, the focus is on the project and the eight parts, and the Alibaba written examinations usually have coding questions, which are rarely asked after an interview, but they ask more questions about principles and often inquire about technology. If you want to interview at a company like ByteDance, the computer base, especially algorithms, is the focus; ByteDance interviews are very code-oriented, and sometimes when you start an interview, you have a coding question to solve. Eight parts of the interview will also be asked, as well as projects, but relatively little. 1. Find more interview experiences, especially from your target company or equivalent.
