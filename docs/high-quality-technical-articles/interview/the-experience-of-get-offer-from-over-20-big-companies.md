In general, Internet company recruitment for technical posts is set up on three to four rounds of interviews, as required, and some of the smaller HC positions may experience five to eight rounds of interviews. In addition, depending on the company, the interview may be preceded by a corresponding written examination.

Multiple rounds of interviews include technical and HR aspects. In relative terms, the technical aspects are more important in the overall recruitment process, and HR is more concerned with identifying the basics of the candidates and their professional qualities.

However, in some large factories, HR also has a veto, so each round of interviews should be prepared for and responded to. Technical interviews can generally be organized into five parts:

1. Introduction by the parties
1. Project experience
1. Examination of expertise
1. Coding capability study
1. Candidates Q&A

# Project Experience

Project experience is a very important part of the interview process, especially in the context of the interview process. The higher the rank, the greater the emphasis on project experience.

For general school admissions, several job matching and project experience with project integrity could be the highlight of the interview and the key to the decision to get `SP' or `SSP'.

But it is not easy to prepare for project experiences. Many do not know how to describe their projects, much less how to reflect their strengths and bright spots in their experiences.

Here are a few suggestions for your project experience:

**1. Efficient and Structured Description**

The project experience is generally the largest part of the curriculum vitae, so it is equally important in interviews. In doing so, the logic and structure of the language must be clear to ensure that the interviewer can capture the overall thinking of your project at the earliest possible time.

It is believed that many have heard of the various principles of CV, such as `STAR', `SMART', etc. But in practice, these principles can be used to regulate their own logic of expression.

The `STAR' principle is relatively simple and is very effective in regulating its own structure during the interview process. The so-called `STAR' is `Situation', `Target', `Action', `Result'. This is broadly consistent with the logic of writing papers and documents.

- `Situation`: i.e., the background of the project, the reasons for the project's presentation, the current status, and the starting point need to be made clear. Simply put, it is to describe the context in which the project is presented. For example, the reason for a particular platform is to address the pain of the user.
- `Target`: i.e., the project's objective, which describes the extent to which the project is expected to be achieved or completed. **It would be preferable to have quantifiable indicators and expected results.** e.g., performance optimization indicators, operational benefits of architecture optimization, etc.
