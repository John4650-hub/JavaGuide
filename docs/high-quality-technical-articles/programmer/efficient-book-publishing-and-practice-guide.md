# 1. Proceeds and Time Required for Book Publication

Let's not talk about the “intangible asset” of the book, but about the real money.

If you contact the publisher directly, the average copy is a royalty, 8% of the book price multiplied by the number of printings (or the actual number of sales), and if you are a big cow, you can add it up, but the general royalties are estimated at between 10 and 12%. Please note that the price here is the full price of the book, not the discount price.

For example, when a book is sold at 70 bucks, and sold at 7 discounts in Kyoto and elsewhere, the royalties are 8% of 70 bucks, which means that selling an author has 5.6 returns, and then deducting taxes after actually getting it.

What difference does it make when I talk to the publisher about the amount printed, usually? Now computer-type books are usually printed in 2500 copies, so the actual money received is 70\*8%\*2500, with tax deductions. But if it's based on the actual number of sales, if it's a copy of 1800, then it's the amount.

Now, a 300-page book, usually priced at about 70, with pre-tax earnings at around 14,000, after-tax estimates at 12,000, and at least eight months for a new author, 300, so you can calculate the average monthly return, which is actually 1,500 per month, which is really small.

I can't tell you what happened to others, but when I published my book, what else would I gain from it?

- When I was interviewed at the current and previous company, and told the interviewer that I had written a book on the subject, the interviewer would immediately think I was senior and save me a lot of money.
- I'm still working on the underline, so I'm just going to use my last Python book as a textbook, so I don't have to do it again.
- To talk to others about the project, to prove their technical strength in my book, and to do so for the first time.

# 2. Time Frame for Payment of Drafts and Benefits After Printing

I'm in direct contact with the publisher, and the time at which I pay a copy is usually a part of a copy (50 to 90 percent) within three months of the original, and then a year after the book is published before the other part. There are many books at the moment, and the number of books that can be written off is not incorrect, but there are many books that can be printed, even in the second and third editions, and the royalties that are usually printed will be settled between six months and one year after the printing.

From the point of time when the draft is paid, there are real delays for the author, and the addition of the draft is not very high, so the book is really not a money-making exercise, and it has a long time to pay. If the individual book company's staff, on the one hand, were not very helpful to the author at the book production stage, and on the other hand, had to make a difference in the middle, it would be a little hard work for the author.
