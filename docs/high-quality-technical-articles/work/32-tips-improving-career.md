# The Shortcut to Growth

- It is good to be modest at the beginning of the job, but do not use “I am new” as a psychological security line.
- It would take about two weeks to write a technical blog, but it is probably the fastest way to grow.
- Two books must be read: Pyramid Principles, Seven Habits of Highly Effective People (the book is like a successful study, actually about how to shape character).
- Ask questions about what, why, the problem is solved by trace, and the problem of trying to bypass will always wait for you at the next intersection.
- Do not become obsessed with the false sense of security that comes from busy work; that is the most real security for targeting and pursuing.
- It should not be too short-lived, and in a fair environment, the loss is not chicken soup.
- Thinking and skills should not be limited to front-end, back-end, and test roles; position yourself as an end-user of business-area issues.
- Curiosity and love are the shortest paths to growth, and long-termists will accept the value of their work, even more than the organization's current endorsement (KPI).

# Kung Fu is on the Day

- Each line of code represents the highest level of your current position; the small details that you feel are irrelevant may hurt you in the promotion arena.
- The biweekly newspaper is not a working day and does not have to be pushed by time; at least know what will happen in the next biweekly newspaper (small target driven).
- Try to classify each of them as small, and imagine that each of them ends with its own goal, so that it is no longer a mechanical requirement, but a planned filling, targeting effort, or even a need for itself, because it sees clearly where it is going.
- Day-to-day speech and behavior are microscopes of ability; most may not be aware, and their strengths and weaknesses are so obvious that no unnecessary attempt is made to cover up, let alone confuse.

The last one would presumably mean that sometimes we care about our performance under the spotlight (statements, promotions, weekly newspapers, debriefings, etc.) and think that people will judge themselves against this. In fact, it is based on how we do business on a day-to-day basis, helping our fellow students, creating value, and the quality of each individual. The partners who have worked together three times can be accurately evaluated, and the performances under the spotlight can only deceive themselves.

# Learn to Be Governed

Superiors, supervisors, etc. are in the general category, as are PD managers who develop their counterparts.

- Do not spread negative sentiments; do not always complain.
- Show greater respect for superiors, but do not publicly rebut each other's views; communicate differences privately.
- Properly manage upwards, especially with respect to expectations and performance communications, which is the responsibility of both parties, but unfortunately for themselves.
- Try to be a supervisor:
  - Understand many of the decisions that seemed so strange in the past.
  - It is far more important to gain the trust of managers in managing the team's concerns than to care about who carries them out or who pays for what.
  - Don't interpret this principle as pre-eminence.

# To Hold on to Your Emotions

- Learn to control emotions; no one listens to what an angry person says.
- Retaliation, anger, and reason should not make you the kind of person who needs to be fooled.
- Self-confident people admit their problems frankly, and we are often provoked by the fact that they point to their deep flaws.
- What hurts us most is not what others do or make, but how we respond.
