# 01 Foreword

You've been working in the Internet for five years, and you look back, and you see where the lights are.

The idea that most programmers who enter the job market will find it hard to learn the technology, and that they will get a better promotion and a higher salary later, is correct, but it is not comprehensive.

At first entry into the workplace, it is felt that hard-working overtime can be constantly improved, and that companies that can learn their skills can accept it even if they have lower salaries. However, five years later, it is believed that overtime is squeezing their own upward space and that low wages are the ceiling of life.

The key point to be made here is that most of the knowledge and ideas of entry into the workplace will no longer apply to knowledge after five years.

The greatest stress after five years of work is choice: the working-field ceiling, the technical-capacity ceiling, the salary ceiling, and the 30-year-old ceiling.

Most programmers are thinking and arguing about how to deal with these problems. The only point of reference for choosing is maximization of the benefits, which can be understood as better promotions in the workplace and being better off.

Over the past five years, the greatest changes have been not in work experience, but rather in the mindset, with a clear understanding of the huge gap between reality and ideal.

# 02 Learn to Adapt to Change and Build Capacity

Looking back at the job world for five years, the most accepted sentence is to learn to adapt to change and to build capacity.

What has changed is that the five-year time frame of technology has been updated, tools have been developed, company environmental teammates have been replaced, and even different cities have been displaced, with the idea of placing the body and soul in one place—a classic phrase: the only thing that remains is the change itself.

What needs to be built up is the ability to solve problems, to think, and to broaden awareness.

Such a description, which is difficult to describe in plain terms, falls within the realm of individual perceptions, and different people have different views, so they can only think from a popular perspective.

First, talking about technology, most of which are small white-levels, want their technological capabilities to grow and to be structured, but in the current Internet environment, this idea is difficult to achieve and is neither a blow nor a challenge.

It's rare to look at the current situation, with 20-30 people in the technical team, 10-15 people in the small group, with an architect dedicated to managing the bottom frame.

The causes of this problem are many, starting with the high cost of the architect, and the environmental architecture, which does not need to be upgraded on a regular basis, may be higher than the project life cycle.

As a result, most of the business of most companies can be addressed on the basis of a majority of existing mature open-source frameworks, which also leads to the role of architects being taken over by project managers or being directly responsible for higher-level development, which is the reality.

This leads to the choice of the technology framework being the right one. This translates into more talent and open-source solutions to reduce the impact of technology on corporate business development.

Then why continue to learn and build technical skills? Without this capacity, the programmer may not have been away for more than five years, and it will be necessary to build up technical expertise to address the problems at work and to raise the level of technical expertise to raise the level of awareness of what is required for his or her operations, which is a fundamental guarantee of physical safety.

This has led to a sharp rise in programmer pressure many five years later, and another barrier to management is business thinking and awareness.

# 03 Increasing the Accumulation of Operational Capacity

Whether or not programmers should focus on the business, there is really no need for a struggle, as long as it is not a purely technical company.

Technology, operations, products, and management are business-oriented.

The most significant change in the five-year career trajectory is the ability to solve operational problems, which, at the outset, is faced with many business scenarios that do not know how to do it, and to design business solutions a few years later.
