# Joining a Satellite Institute in Shanghai

I was born in '86 and graduated in '13 from a medium-sized software-related program. Since both parents are teachers, education from an early age was an effort to learn and find a stable “good job” to serve the country.

So, when I graduated, I joined a satellite institute at Shanghai Spacex College and decided on my first job hastily, without thinking about it, just by listening to my parents, which also left me with a serious problem five years later. Here is the first lesson:

**If your family is in the general class, then for some of the big things in life, they tend to give advice to the general class, which is the result of their life-long thinking decisions, and if your goal is to get out of the class, it is best to take their advice as a reference.**

In April 2013, I got on a train to Shanghai, went all the way to Tai Po, walked out of the subway to work, and the buildings were older on the way, and I thought it was no different from my home.

It was reported that the teacher in charge of reporting was very kind, filled out the information, distributed a dormitory, and paid about 3k or so to settle the house, which I was so excited about. I took the money, bought food at the nearby supermarket, and so I started my own space career.

After a month of intensive training, I was assigned to the Department, which is primarily responsible for the development of embedded software on satellites. But it's the development of high-rise satellite software, which in fact starts with handing over equipment to laboratories and factories. At that time, however, young people had a very simple mind, moving their equipment every day, working overtime at night, reading documents, drawing software flow charts, programming exercises, and living well.

Remember that the first month was about 5k, when a lot of colleagues who joined the job complained that I didn't, and I didn't even want to compare their wages, and here's the second lesson:

**Do not compare wages to those of your colleagues. It doesn't make sense.**

# Show Your Head at Work

When I was working for about a month, I met something, and I started showing off among the new staff. This is what happened at the time when the state had to give GJB5000A software development certification to the military engineering unit (a student who had this certification should have known how sour and sour it was), but then a colleague in charge of configuration management offered to leave because he was in the civil service, and the backstage of the software platform we used was SVN, which I used to write the program at school. I remember being laughed at by my classmates, which reminds me of the story of Jobos studying art.

**Don't say that a skill is useless. Any skill you have has value, but you have to learn to play it. If one day you get in the water, you might be glad you learned to swim.**
