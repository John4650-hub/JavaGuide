# Learn to Think Deep and Sum Up the Sediment

**The first thing I want to say is to learn to think in depth and to sum up the sediment, which I think is the most important and meaningful thing.**

**Let's start with an in-depth reflection.** In the circle of programmers, there's a lot of talk: _ _ "I have no technical skills in this job, write if-else every day, what the heck can I learn?" \_

It may indeed be the real thinking of some of our classmates, at least once, that I did. And then, with work experience and some high-level class members, I found it really wrong. The perception that there is nothing to learn is largely the result of lazy thinking. **Any small matter that appears to be insensitive is a knowledge-based ocean that can be insinuated by in-depth thinking, a slightly vertical dig or a horizontal expansion.**

Take one example. One time, a fellow student told me that there was a service this week, looked into a place where defer wrote a problem, changed several lines of code to fix, and the weekly paper couldn't write. This may have been the case for all of us, and it would have been quite representative. In fact, in the case of the bug, it is a process of finding problems, sorting out problems, solving problems, including triggers, locating, re-emerging, root causes, repairing, and rediscovering, which takes a week to do this, and there must be an ongoing process of trying and correcting mistakes, and there is much room for reflection. How to narrow down, for example? Which bends? What analytical tools are used? For example, root causes, at least Linux OOM, k8s OOM, Go memory management, defer mechanism, the principle of a function closed. If this really doesn't involve it, it still takes a week to do it.

**Summarized sedimentation.** This is also the place where most programmers, I think, are less well-equipped to work on their heads, and can do one thing well. It is important to take stock of the sediment at the right time, a process from the trick to the path that allows for a broader perspective and a higher level.
